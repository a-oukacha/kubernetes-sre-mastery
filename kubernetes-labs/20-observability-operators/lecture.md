## What just happened (under the hood)

**The four pillars of observability.** *Logs* (discrete events, ideally structured with a request ID), *metrics* (cheap numeric time series - what you used here), *traces* (one request's causal path across services), *alerts* (a metric/trace condition that demands human or automated action). They are complementary: metrics tell you *something* is wrong cheaply and always-on; traces tell you *where*; logs tell you *exactly what*; alerts decide *when a human cares*. You instrument for all four but you *page* on a tiny curated subset.

RED vs USE - two lenses, two subjects. **RED** (Rate, Errors, Duration) describes a *request-driven service* from the caller's side - it is what your users feel, so it is what your SLOs are built from. **USE** (Utilization, Saturation, Errors) describes a *resource* (CPU, disk, a queue) from the inside - it explains *why* RED went bad. RED for the symptom, USE for the cause. The CPU alert in Break-it B3 was a USE signal mis-promoted to a pager; that is the classic mistake.

The pull model and the Operator that feeds it. Prometheus *scrapes* `/metrics` on an interval rather than receiving pushes. Pull means the monitoring system controls cardinality and cadence, and a target that disappears is *itself* a signal (its `up` series goes to 0). You never wrote `prometheus.yml`: the **Prometheus Operator** watches `ServiceMonitor`/`PrometheusRule` CRDs and reconciles them into the running server's config and rules. That is the same machine you built in steps 8-11, just shipped by someone else.

SLI -> SLO -> error budget - the chain. An **SLI** is a *measured ratio of good events to total events* - here, non-5xx ÷ all requests. An **SLO** is the *target* for that ratio over a window - 99% over 30 days. The **error budget** is `1 − SLO` - the 1% you are *allowed* to fail. The budget reframes reliability from "never fail" (impossible, and the wrong goal) to "fail no faster than this." Crucially the budget is a *resource you spend on velocity*: if you have budget to spare, ship faster / take more risk; if you have burned it, freeze and stabilize. The number is a contract, not a vibe.

Multi-window, multi-burn-rate alerting. A naive alert ("error ratio > 1% for 5m") either pages on every blip or misses slow bleeds. **Burn rate** = how fast you are consuming the budget relative to "evenly over the window." Burn rate 1 = you will exactly exhaust the budget at the window's end; 14.4 = you will exhaust a 30-day budget in ~2 days. You alert at *multiple* burn rates, each with a *short confirmation window paired with a longer one*: the long window gives significance (not a blip), the short window gives fast detection and fast reset. Fast burn (14.4× / 1h+5m) **pages**; slow burn (3× / 6h+30m) **tickets**. This is the Google SRE Workbook recipe, and it is strictly better than a static threshold on both false-positive and false-negative axes.

Traces show causality; metrics show aggregates. A latency histogram tells you p99 rose; it cannot tell you *which* of five downstream calls caused it. A trace propagates a context ID across service hops (W3C `traceparent` header) so a single slow request becomes a tree of timed spans. You saw the concept with `/delay/1`: the second lived in the downstream span. Tracing's cost is context propagation discipline in code and a collector to store spans (Tempo/Jaeger) - which is why it is the pillar teams adopt last.

**The reconcile loop is *all* of Kubernetes. Every controller - Deployment, ReplicaSet, the scheduler, kube-proxy, the Prometheus Operator, and your 60-line bash loop - runs the same cycle: observe** actual state, **diff** it against desired state, **act** to close the gap, repeat forever. It is *level-triggered* (it reacts to the current state, not to a stream of edge events), which is why it is self-healing and idempotent: a missed event, a hand-deleted child, a controller restart - all converge on the next pass. An **Operator** is nothing more than *your* reconcile loop over *your* CRD: you encode "how to operate this thing" (lab 13's adapter knowledge, lab 11's leader election, a database's backup/failover) as software so a human never does it by hand again. The CRD is the API; the controller is the SRE-in-a-box.

## Dev notes
- **Instrument for RED, in the app. Expose a request counter labeled by route+status (Rate, Errors) and a latency histogram (Duration - never a gauge of "current latency"; you need buckets to compute percentiles server-side). podinfo did this for you; for your own service it is a few lines with the Prometheus client library, or the lab-13 adapter** if you cannot touch the code.
- **Propagate trace context.** Accept and forward the `traceparent` header on every outbound call. A trace is only as deep as the least-instrumented hop - one service that drops the header blinds the whole tree below it.
- **Structured logs with a request ID.** Log JSON, include the trace/request ID, and you can pivot from an alert -> a trace -> the exact log lines for that one request. Free-text logs without an ID are a grep-and-pray exercise during an incident.
- **Don't hand-roll retries.** Use the client library's backoff+jitter+breaker (gRPC retry policy, AWS SDK adaptive retries, resilience4j, Polly). A bare `while: call; sleep(const)` is a thundering herd waiting for an outage.

## DevOps / Platform notes
- **The stack:** Prometheus (metrics) + Grafana (dashboards) + Alertmanager (routing/dedup/silence) + Loki (logs) + Tempo/Jaeger (traces), wired by the Prometheus Operator via `ServiceMonitor`/`PodMonitor`/`PrometheusRule`. Provide it as a platform service so app teams only ship `/metrics` + a `ServiceMonitor`.
- **Dashboards and alerts as code.** Keep Grafana dashboards as JSON and `PrometheusRule`s in Git, reviewed and deployed like any manifest. A dashboard edited in the UI is drift (lab 01's lesson) and is gone when the pod restarts.
- Alertmanager routing is its own discipline: route `severity=page` to the on-call pager and `severity=ticket` to a queue; group related alerts so one incident is one page, not fifty; inhibition so a "cluster down" alert suppresses the hundred "pod unreachable" children; maintenance silences so planned work does not page.
- **PodMonitor vs ServiceMonitor:** ServiceMonitor scrapes via a Service's endpoints (the common case); PodMonitor scrapes pods directly (for things behind no Service, like Jobs).

## Architect notes (trade-offs)
- Observability is a system property, designed in - not bolted on. You cannot add cardinality-safe metrics, trace propagation, and request IDs to a running incident. Decide the metric names, label sets, and the SLI definition *before* launch; they are an API your dashboards and alerts depend on.
- When to build an operator - and when not to. Build one when you have recurring operational toil over a domain object that is hard to express as plain manifests: stateful failover, backup/restore, certificate rotation, multi-step upgrades (this is what the etcd, Postgres, and Prometheus operators encode). *Don't* build one when a Helm chart + a CronJob + a `kubectl apply` in CI already does the job - an operator is a long-lived privileged controller you now have to run, secure, and debug. The cost is real; pay it only to retire real, repeated toil.
- **Operator vs external automation.** An operator lives *in* the cluster and reconciles continuously (self-healing, fast). External automation (CI pipeline, Terraform, a Lambda) runs *outside* on a trigger or schedule (no standing privilege, easier to reason about, but edge-triggered and slower to converge). In-cluster continuous control buys you self-healing at the price of a privileged always-on component.
- Cardinality is an architectural budget. Every distinct label-value combination is a separate time series. A `user_id` or `request_id` label will explode Prometheus's memory and can take the monitoring system down *during* the incident you needed it for. Labels are for *bounded* dimensions (route, status, version), never for unbounded identifiers - those go in logs/traces.

## SRE notes (failure modes, SLOs, toil - the capstone lens)

This is where every prior lab converges. SRE is the discipline of running services to an *agreed* reliability target, treating reliability as an engineering quantity you measure, budget, and spend.

**SLI/SLO/error budget is the reliability *contract* between dev and ops.** Without it, "is it reliable enough?" is an argument; with it, it is a number both sides agreed to. The SLO defines "good enough," the budget quantifies the allowed unreliability, and the burn rate is the live gauge. The most important property of the budget is that it is *shared*: dev and ops have the same incentive - dev wants to ship (spend budget on velocity), ops wants stability (preserve budget); the budget makes the trade-off explicit instead of political. Error budgets gate release velocity: budget remaining -> ship freely, take risks, do chaos experiments; budget exhausted -> automatic change freeze until you have earned it back. This turns "should we deploy on Friday?" into a lookup.

Alert on symptoms, not causes (Break-it B3, in production terms). Page on user-facing SLO burn - the thing your users actually feel. *Causes* (high CPU, full disk, restarting pods) belong on dashboards and in runbooks for *diagnosis after the page*, not on the pager itself: they false-alarm (a healthy service can be CPU-busy) and they miss (a logic bug serving 100% 500s may use no CPU at all). A page must mean "a human's attention will change the user outcome." Every page that does not meet that bar erodes the on-call's trust and trains them to ignore the pager - the real cause of missed incidents.

"The absence of errors is an error" - alert on no-data. A burn-rate alert needs traffic to fire. If your scrape target vanishes, the load balancer black-holes requests, or a Job stops producing, the error *ratio* may read as "fine" because the denominator went to zero. You must *also* alert on zero traffic / no data / `up == 0` / stale series for things that should always be busy. The scariest outage is the silent one where the dashboard is green because nothing is being measured. Pull-based monitoring helps here (a missing target is visible), but you still need the explicit "this should never be quiet" alert.

The failure vocabulary (DDS ch16) you now have hands-on.
- Thundering herd / retry amplification (Break-it B1): synchronized retries pin load at full amplitude and prevent recovery. Fix: exponential **backoff** (load decays) + **jitter** (waves desynchronize). This same herd appears at *cold start* (lab 18 - every replica of a freshly scaled service hits a cold cache/dependency at once) and at *deploy* (all pods reconnect simultaneously).
- **Circuit breaker:** after N consecutive failures, *stop calling* (open) for a cooldown, then send one trial (half-open). It protects both the caller (fail fast instead of hanging) and the callee (gets breathing room). Envoy/the ambassador (lab 13) gives you this without app changes.
- **Cascading failure:** one saturated service times out its callers, which retry, which saturate *their* callers - the failure climbs the dependency graph. Backoff+jitter+breakers + load shedding + bounded queues contain it; edge rate-limiting (lab 07) caps it.
- **Processing obsolete work: after an outage, a backlog of now-stale work (alerts for a node already replaced, jobs for a deploy already rolled back) floods in. Triage newest-first**, set TTLs (lab 16's `ttlSecondsAfterFinished`), and make work idempotent so re-processing is safe.

**Toil reduction via operators.** Toil is manual, repetitive, automatable operational work that scales with the service and produces no lasting value. The Website controller is the antidote in miniature: it took a multi-step manual procedure ("to stand up a site, write a Deployment, write a Service, wire them, watch them") and encoded it once. Every operator is a toil-elimination project - the cost-justification is "how many 3am pages and manual runbooks does this retire?"

On-call hygiene and blameless postmortems. A healthy on-call rotation pages rarely (every page is actionable and symptom-based), hands off cleanly, and has a runbook per alert. Every page that fires without a human-fixable cause is a bug in your alerting to be fixed, not endured. After an incident, run a **blameless postmortem**: the question is "what about the *system* (including our tooling, alerts, and defaults) let this happen and let it hurt," never "who typed the bad command." Blame drives the truth underground; you need the truth to fix the system. The output is action items that reduce recurrence or blast radius - often a new SLO, a better symptom alert, a backoff fix, or an operator.

**This lab ties to every prior one.** Probes (lab 03) are the per-pod health signal Endpoints and the scheduler read; PDBs (lab 11) bound the *availability* you are SLO-ing during voluntary disruption; leader election (lab 11) is why your controller runs as a singleton; autoscaling latency (lab 18) is a direct SLO input (scale-up lag *is* tail latency, and cold-start is a thundering herd); RBAC (lab 19) is why the controller's ServiceAccount gets exactly the verbs it needs and no more; the adapter (lab 13) is how a non-instrumented app even gets a `/metrics` for you to SLO. Reliability is not a feature you add at the end; it is the sum of decisions made in every lab before this one.

## AI/ML notes (LLM/ML serving mapping - conceptual)
- Inference SLIs are latency-shaped and token-aware. The RED triad becomes: **TTFT (time-to-first-token - the user-perceived "did it start"), TPOT / inter-token latency** (streaming smoothness), **p99 end-to-end** per request, **throughput** in tokens/sec, and **queue depth / wait time** (requests parked before a slot frees). An LLM SLO is typically "p95 TTFT < X and p99 end-to-end < Y" - and you alert on *those* burn rates, not on raw GPU%.
- **USE for accelerators via DCGM. GPU utilization, memory (VRAM) used vs total, SM occupancy, ECC errors, and power/thermal come from the DCGM exporter** (a DaemonSet, lab 15 - concept only; no GPU in this course). VRAM is an *incompressible* resource like RAM (lab 05): exceed it and you OOM, you do not throttle. KV-cache growth is the usual culprit.
- Model-serving operators are the Website controller at production scale. **KServe's `InferenceService`** CR is exactly your `Website` pattern: a user declares "serve model X with this runtime and autoscaling," and the operator materializes the predictor Deployment, the Service, the autoscaler, the routing, and the storage mount. The operator encodes *model-deployment ops knowledge* (warm-up, scale-to-zero, canary between model versions) as a reconcile loop - toil elimination for ML platform teams.
- **Quality SLIs beyond latency. Inference has signals classic services lack: accuracy / eval-score**, **drift (input distribution moving away from training), hallucination / guardrail-violation rate**, and **cost-per-token** (an efficiency SLI - tokens/sec/dollar). These ride alongside latency SLOs; a model can be fast and *wrong*, which a pure RED dashboard would call healthy.

## Pitfalls
- **Alerting on causes, not symptoms** - pages that false-alarm on healthy load and miss cheap-to-serve outages (Break-it B3). Page on SLO burn; dashboard the causes.
- **Retries without backoff/jitter** - the thundering herd you reproduced (B1). Naive retries *amplify* outages. Always backoff + jitter + breaker.
- **Logging everything** - unbounded log volume and cost, and the signal drowns in noise. Log decisions and errors with IDs; sample the rest.
- **SLOs no one agreed to** - an SLO invented by one team is ignored by the other. The number must be a *negotiated contract* with consequences (the budget gating velocity), or it is decoration.
- A buggy reconcile loop fighting itself - a controller whose "act" step changes state its "observe" step then reacts to (e.g. it edits a field it also watches) oscillates forever, churning the API server. Make reconciliation idempotent and converge to a fixed point; use owner references + generation tracking in real operators.
- **Cardinality explosions** - a high-cardinality label (`user_id`, `request_id`, full URL) multiplies time series until Prometheus OOMs - taking down monitoring during the incident you built it for. Bounded labels only.
- Forgetting the CRD is cluster-scoped - deleting the namespace leaves `websites.example.com` behind (and any ClusterRole/Binding a real operator would use). Delete cluster-scoped objects explicitly (lab Cleanup).
