# Lecture 08 - NetworkPolicy & segmentation: from flat allow-all to least connectivity

## Answers to the lab checkpoints
- **(0)** Yes, `frontend` can reach `db` right now - and no, it absolutely shouldn't in production. With zero policies, Kubernetes' network is **flat and allow-all**: every pod can open a connection to every other pod's IP, in any namespace. That convenience is the default; segmentation is something you must *add*.
- **(1) With the probe applied, `backend->db` must time out**. The probe selects only `tier=db` and grants it *no* ingress rules, which on an enforcing CNI means **deny-all ingress to db**. If db still answers, the CNI is parsing the object but not enforcing it - the policy is a no-op and so will be every policy in the lab. Enforcement is the CNI's job; the API server happily stores policies a non-enforcing CNI ignores.
- **(2)** All three hops succeed, including `frontend->db`. This is the baseline flat network: no policy selects any pod, so nothing is denied. You're looking at the exact over-connectivity that lets a compromised edge pod walk straight to the database.
- **(3)** None of the inter-pod hops still work. `podSelector: {}` selects *every* pod in the namespace; `policyTypes: [Ingress]` with no `ingress:` block means "deny all ingress" for each selected pod. A pod becomes default-deny for the direction(s) named in `policyTypes` the moment *any* policy selects it. (Egress is untouched here, so pods can still *initiate* outbound - but nothing can initiate *inbound* to them.)
- **(4) `frontend->backend` and `backend->db` return; `frontend->db` still times out.** Policies are **additive allow-lists** that *union* together - there is no deny rule, only the presence or absence of an allow. Nobody wrote a `frontend->db` allow, so the default-deny that the ingress policy imposed on db stands. That blocked hop *is* least connectivity.
- **(5)** No - `frontend->backend` breaks again. `wget backend` must first **resolve the name `backend`** via a DNS query to kube-dns (UDP/TCP :53). Default-deny egress blocks that outbound DNS query, so the client never gets an IP to connect to. The ingress allow on backend is irrelevant: the connection is never attempted because the *lookup* failed. (Even with a literal IP, the outbound TCP would also be denied until step 6's egress allow.)
- **(6)** Both hops recover. `31-allow-dns-egress` re-opens `:53` to kube-dns so names resolve; `32-allow-tier-egress` re-opens the outbound TCP for the two intended hops. No rule grants `0.0.0.0/0`, so the internet stays unreachable - the pods have exactly the connectivity they need.
- **(B1)** It fails as a **name-resolution failure**, not a clean TCP timeout to backend. You'll see a DNS/lookup error (`bad address`, `wget: bad address 'backend'`, or a lookup timeout) - symptoms that look like "the app is broken" or "the Service is gone," which is exactly why this incident is so often misdiagnosed.
- **(B2) `nslookup backend` fails because default-deny egress dropped the `:53` query to kube-dns. The ingress allow `frontend->backend` is still present and still correct - it just can't help, because the client can't discover backend's address. Dialing the fully-qualified name** doesn't help either; FQDN vs short name is a *search-domain* nuance, not the cause - DNS itself is blocked.
- **(B3) One rule fixes it: egress to kube-dns on `53/udp` + `53/tcp`. The instant that policy lands, lookups succeed and traffic flows. Memorize this: a default-deny egress policy is incomplete without a DNS-egress allow.**

## What just happened (under the hood)
You walked the canonical segmentation progression: default-allow -> default-deny -> additive allow-list, in both directions.

1. The default is a flat, allow-all network. Kubernetes' networking model guarantees every pod gets a routable IP and that *any pod can reach any pod without NAT* - including across namespaces. There is no implicit firewall. Namespaces scope *names, RBAC, and quotas*, but **not** network reachability.
2. Selecting a pod flips it to default-deny - for the selected direction only. A `NetworkPolicy` does two things at once. Its `podSelector` chooses which pods it governs; its `policyTypes` (`Ingress`, `Egress`, or both) declares which directions become **default-deny** for those pods. The moment *any* policy selects a pod for a direction, that pod denies all traffic in that direction *except* what some policy explicitly allows. A pod never selected by any policy keeps the flat allow-all behavior.
3. Policies are additive allow-lists - a union, with no deny. There is deliberately **no "deny" rule** in `networking.k8s.io/v1`. You cannot write "block frontend->db." You make a hop impossible by *not allowing it* once default-deny is in force. Multiple policies that select the same pod **union** their allows together (logical OR). This is why segmentation is built by *subtraction-via-omission*: deny-all, then add back exactly the hops you need.
4. Enforcement is the CNI's job - kube-proxy/iptables doesn't do it. The Service VIP plumbing you saw in lab 06 is kube-proxy; NetworkPolicy is a *separate* concern enforced by the CNI's datapath (Calico's Felix/iptables/eBPF, Cilium's eBPF, the VPC CNI's network-policy agent). A CNI that doesn't implement NetworkPolicy (or has it disabled) makes every policy a **silent no-op**. The object exists, `kubectl get netpol` shows it, and *nothing is enforced*. That is why Step 1 was non-negotiable.
5. The DNS trap is structural, not a bug. Service discovery is DNS (lab 06). DNS is *egress* to kube-dns on :53. A blanket default-deny egress therefore severs name resolution for every selected pod - the failure surfaces as "the app can't find its dependencies," far from the policy you just applied. The fix is a tiny, explicit DNS-egress allow that every default-deny-egress posture must include.
6. **This is L3/L4 only.** A NetworkPolicy reasons about IPs, ports, and pod/namespace labels - layers 3 and 4. It cannot say "allow `GET /healthz` but not `POST /admin`," and it does not authenticate the *identity* of the caller (IP can be spoofed/reused). Identity-aware L7 rules and **mTLS** are the domain of a service mesh (Cilium's L7 policy, Istio, Linkerd) - a different tool layered on top.

## Dev notes
- Declare the connectivity your service needs - don't assume an open network. Ship a NetworkPolicy *with* your app that names its real dependencies (its DB, its cache, kube-dns). Treat "what can this talk to" as part of the API contract, like ports and env vars.
- Your client code does DNS before it does TCP. Almost every "connection" is actually *resolve, then connect*. When you lock down egress, you must allow `:53` to kube-dns or nothing your code dials by name will work - and the error you get back will *look* like the dependency is down.
- **Label for selection, deliberately.** Policies select on labels (`tier: backend`). Sloppy or missing labels mean your policy silently governs the wrong set - or nothing. The `tier:` labels here are the join key the whole segmentation rests on.

## DevOps / Platform notes
- **CNI choice GATES the feature.** Before promising "we have network segmentation," confirm the CNI enforces it: EKS needs VPC CNI network policy enabled *or* Calico; OVH MKS is Cilium/Canal (verify per version). A policy on a non-enforcing CNI is theater. Bake an enforcement probe (this lab's Step 1) into platform smoke tests.
- **Test policies in CI.** Connectivity is easy to break and silent when over-permissive. Tools like `cyclonus`, Cilium's `connectivity test`, or a simple matrix of `kubectl exec ... wget` pods (allowed=succeeds, forbidden=times out) catch regressions. Ship a default-deny baseline per namespace and let app teams add allows.
- **Namespace-scoped vs cluster-wide.** Core `NetworkPolicy` is namespaced - each team owns its own. For cluster-wide guardrails (e.g. "no pod may egress to the internet," "deny cross-namespace by default") use the CNI's CRDs: Calico `GlobalNetworkPolicy`, Cilium `CiliumClusterwideNetworkPolicy`. Those are vendor extensions, not portable.

## Architect notes (trade-offs)
- Zero-trust networking / microsegmentation. The flat default is "anyone can call anyone." Least connectivity inverts that to "nothing talks unless explicitly allowed." NetworkPolicy is the in-cluster primitive for it.
- **Blast-radius containment.** The headline result - `frontend->db` blocked while `frontend->backend->db` flows - means a compromised edge pod *cannot pivot directly to the data tier*. It must go through the backend, which you can harden, audit, and rate-limit. Segmentation turns one breached pod into a contained incident instead of a clear path to the crown jewels.
- **Where mesh/mTLS earns its place.** NetworkPolicy authorizes by *IP/label/port* - it can't verify *who* the caller really is, and it's blind to L7. When you need cryptographic workload identity (SPIFFE), per-route authorization, or encryption in transit between services, a mesh (Istio/Linkerd) or Cilium L7 adds that *on top of* L3/L4 segmentation. Use NetworkPolicy as the coarse, cheap, always-on baseline; reach for mesh when identity and L7 matter - not as a replacement.

## SRE notes (failure modes, SLOs, toil)
- THE classic gotcha: default-deny egress without a DNS allow. It severs name resolution for every selected pod and presents as a broad, confusing app outage ("everything can't reach its dependencies") with no obvious link to the policy change. First question after any egress-policy rollout: *did we allow :53 to kube-dns?*
- Silent non-enforcement = false security. A wrong/disabled CNI means your policies don't apply and your app keeps working - so nobody notices until an audit or an incident. Periodically run an enforcement probe; treat "policy exists" and "policy enforced" as two separate facts.
- **Segmentation vs debuggability.** Tight egress breaks the engineer's reflexes: `kubectl exec ... curl somewhere` now fails *by design*, and "is it the app or the policy?" becomes the recurring 3am question. Debug it methodically: reproduce **with and without** the suspect policy (`kubectl delete netpol X`, retest, re-apply), use the short *and* FQDN to separate DNS from connectivity, and read the CNI's drop logs (Calico/Cilium can log denied flows; `cilium monitor --type drop`, `hubble observe --verdict DROPPED`). The symptom - timeout vs NXDOMAIN vs connection-refused - tells you the layer.
- Order of operations matters in rollout. Apply allow-lists *before* (or together with) default-deny, and always include the DNS allow in the same change, or you'll bounce traffic. Stage in a canary namespace.

## AI/ML notes (LLM/ML serving mapping - conceptual)
- Isolate the model/data plane from the public API plane. Put the inference servers, the **model registry**, and the **vector DB** behind policies that admit traffic *only* from the API gateway / orchestrator tier - the same `frontend->backend->db` shape, where "db" is your weights store or feature store. The public-facing API pod should never be able to reach the weight store directly.
- Egress control to prevent exfiltration. A compromised inference pod that can reach `0.0.0.0/0` can POST your model weights or training data to the internet. A default-deny egress that allows only kube-dns + the internal model/registry hops makes weight/data exfiltration structurally hard - arguably the highest-leverage policy on an ML platform. (Same posture you proved by leaving `0.0.0.0/0` unallowed in this lab.)
- Restrict who can reach the registry / vector DB. Only the loader/serving pods need the model registry; only the retriever needs the vector DB. Encode that as allow-lists so a breached sidecar or notebook pod can't pull proprietary weights.
- mTLS between inference microservices (router -> KV-cache -> workers) is the L7/identity layer *above* this: NetworkPolicy says "these pods may talk on this port"; a mesh adds "and they prove cryptographically who they are, encrypted." Both, layered. (All conceptual - no GPU needed; the mechanics are identical to the agnhost tiers you ran.)

## Pitfalls
- Default-deny egress without a DNS allow -> cluster-name resolution dies for selected pods; looks like an app outage. The #1 NetworkPolicy incident.
- **Assuming a policy is enforced** when the CNI ignores it -> policies exist, nothing bites, false security. Probe enforcement explicitly.
- **Forgetting policies are allow-only** -> people try to "deny frontend->db" and there is no such rule; you block by *omitting* the allow under a default-deny, not by writing a deny.
- `namespaceSelector` vs `podSelector` confusion -> and the AND/OR subtlety: two separate entries under `from:`/`to:` are **OR**; a `namespaceSelector` and `podSelector` *nested in the same entry* are **AND** ("this pod in that namespace"). Getting this wrong silently over- or under-permits. The DNS-egress policy here deliberately uses the AND form to pin kube-dns in kube-system.
- **Policy targets the wrong pods** -> an empty `podSelector: {}` is namespace-wide (powerful, easy to over-apply); a typo'd label selects nothing and silently does nothing.
